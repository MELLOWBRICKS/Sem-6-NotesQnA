Q1. Explain Software Configuration Management (SCM).

Definition:

Software Configuration Management (SCM) is the process of tracking and controlling changes in software during development and maintenance. It ensures software consistency, integrity, and traceability.

Key Functions of SCM:

  1. Identification: Recognizing and defining software components (files, documents, code versions).
  1. Version Control: Managing multiple versions of software to track modifications.
  1. Change Control: Monitoring and approving modifications before implementation.
  1. Configuration Audit: Ensuring implemented changes meet requirements and standards.
  1. Status Reporting: Providing updates on changes through user guides, FAQs, and release notes.

Benefits of SCM:

  • Prevents unauthorized changes to software.
  • Ensures team collaboration without conflicts.
  • Improves traceability and rollback capability in case of errors.

Q2. Define Defect Prevention and Explain Peer Reviews in Detail.

Defect Prevention (DP):

  • A proactive approach to prevent previously detected defects from recurring.
  • Involves identifying root causes and eliminating them at the source.
  • Conducted through reviews, static analysis, and process improvements.

Peer Reviews:

  • Definition: A systematic examination of software by team members to detect defects early.
  • Purpose: Improve software quality by reviewing design, code, or test cases.
  • Types of Peer Reviews:
    1. Formal Review: Structured, follows a process, involves inspection and walkthroughs.
    1. Informal Review: Casual discussions without documentation, helps identify minor issues early.

Steps in Peer Review Process:

  1. Planning: Identify objectives and assign roles (author, reviewer, moderator).
  1. Preparation: Reviewers analyze the artifact before the meeting.
  1. Review Meeting: Reviewers discuss findings and document defects.
  1. Rework: The author fixes defects.
  1. Follow-up: Ensures all defects are resolved before closure.

Q3. Explain the following Quality Tools:

1. Pareto Chart:

  • A bar chart that represents data in descending order with a cumulative percentage line.
  • Based on the Pareto Principle (80/20 rule): 80% of problems come from 20% of causes.

Use Case: Identifying major defects affecting software quality and prioritizing them.

2. Scatter Diagram:

  • A graphical representation of two variables to identify correlations.
  • Example: A scatter diagram can show the relationship between defect count and testing time.

Use Case: Helps in root cause analysis by finding dependencies between variables.

Q4. Explain CMM Levels.

Capability Maturity Model (CMM) is a framework for assessing and improving software development processes.

Five CMM Levels:

  1. Level 1 – Initial:
    • No defined processes, development is unpredictable.
    • Example: Startups with ad-hoc development.
  1. Level 2 – Repeatable:
    • Basic project management practices in place.
    • Example: Version control, requirement tracking.
  1. Level 3 – Defined:
    • Organization-wide process standardization.
    • Example: Formal SQA, process documentation.
  1. Level 4 – Managed:
    • Quantitative process control using metrics.
    • Example: Defect density monitoring, statistical quality control.
  1. Level 5 – Optimizing:
    • Continuous process improvement and innovation.
    • Example: Six Sigma, process automation.

Q6. Explain Requirement Management.

Definition:

Requirement Management ensures that software meets business needs by tracking and managing all requirements throughout the development lifecycle.

Key Steps in Requirement Management:

  1. Requirement Elicitation: Gathering requirements from stakeholders.
  1. Requirement Analysis: Identifying conflicts and dependencies.
  1. Requirement Documentation: Writing SRS (Software Requirement Specification).
  1. Requirement Validation: Ensuring completeness and feasibility.
  1. Requirement Traceability: Mapping each requirement to design, coding, and testing.

Benefits of Requirement Management:

  • Prevents requirement mismatches and scope creep.
  • Enhances customer satisfaction by ensuring correct implementation.

Q7. Explain Software Product Engineering.

Software Product Engineering (SPE) refers to the complete process of designing, developing, testing, and maintaining software products.

Key Aspects of SPE:

  1. Quality: Ensuring high standards in design and development.
  1. Usability: Making the software easy to use.
  1. Functionality: Ensuring all features work as required.
  1. Durability: Building software that can handle long-term use.
  1. Validation: Ensuring that the final product meets user needs.
  1. Compliance: Following industry regulations and standards.
  1. Documentation: Creating manuals and user guides.

Q8. What is a Cause-Effect Diagram? When to use a Cause-Effect Diagram? Give Advantages of It.

Cause-Effect Diagram (Fishbone or Ishikawa Diagram):

  • A visual tool used to identify root causes of problems.
  • Categorizes causes into Materials, Methods, Equipment, Environment, People.

When to Use a Cause-Effect Diagram?

  • When analyzing defect causes in software.
  • When brainstorming improvements for a process.

Advantages:

  1. Identifies key areas for further investigation.
  1. Encourages team participation in problem-solving.
  1. Provides a structured format to analyze defects.
  1. Visual representation makes it easy to understand.
  1. Helps in root cause analysis and corrective actions.

Q9. Explain Defect Life Cycle.

Definition:

The Defect Life Cycle (Bug Life Cycle) defines the different stages a defect goes through from identification to closure.

Phases of Defect Life Cycle:

  1. New: A defect is identified and logged.
  1. Assigned: The defect is assigned to a developer.
  1. Open: The developer starts analyzing and fixing the defect.
  1. Fixed: The defect is resolved.
  1. Retest: The tester verifies the fix.
  1. Verified: If the fix works, the defect is marked as verified.
  1. Closed: The defect is considered resolved and closed.
  1. Reopened (if necessary): If the issue persists, it is reopened for further fixes.

Diagram Representation:

graph TD;
  A[New] --> B[Assigned]
  B --> C[Open]
  C --> D[Fixed]
  D --> E[Retest]
  E --> F[Verified]
  F --> G[Closed]
  E -->|If defect not fixed| H[Reopened]
  H --> C

Importance of Defect Life Cycle:

  • Helps in tracking defects efficiently.
  • Ensures timely resolution of issues.
  • Improves software quality by maintaining defect records.
