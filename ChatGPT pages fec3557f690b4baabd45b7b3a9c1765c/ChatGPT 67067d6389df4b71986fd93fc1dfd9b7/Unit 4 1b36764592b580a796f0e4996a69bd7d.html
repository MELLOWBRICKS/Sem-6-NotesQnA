Q2. What is a Regression Tree? Enlist the Advantages and Usage.

Definition

A Regression Tree is a decision-tree-based model used for regression tasks, where the target variable is continuous. It splits the data into subsets using decision rules and predicts the output as the average value of data points in each leaf node.

Advantages

  1. Easy Interpretation – Can be visualized for decision-making.
  1. Handles Non-Linearity – Works well for complex relationships.
  1. Feature Selection – Automatically selects important features.
  1. No Need for Scaling – Works well with raw data without normalization.
  1. Handles Missing Data – Can manage missing values efficiently.

Usage

  • Predicting house prices based on features like size, location, and amenities.
  • Estimating customer lifetime value in business analytics.
  • Forecasting sales trends based on historical data.

  • Example: CART (Classification and Regression Trees) can be adapted to create clusters of customers based on purchasing behavior.
  • Use Case: Identifying customer segments in a retail business for targeted marketing.

Q4. Finding Dependence of Butter and Jam on Bread

We will determine whether butter and jam are dependent on bread using Support, Confidence, and Lift in Association Rule Mining.

Given Data:

  • Total Customers = 2500
  • Customers Buying Bread (B) = 560
  • Customers Buying Butter (But) = 350
  • Customers Buying Jam (Jam) = 470
  • Customers Buying Both Bread & Butter (B ∩ But) = 250
  • Customers Buying Both Bread & Jam (B ∩ Jam) = 300

Step 4: Conclusion

  • Since Lift > 1 for both Butter and Jam, they are both positively dependent on Bread.
  • Bread → Butter (Lift = 3.18) has a stronger dependency than Bread → Jam (Lift = 2.85).
  • Final Answer: Butter is more dependent on Bread than Jam. ✅

Q5. What is a Decision Tree?

A Decision Tree is a supervised learning algorithm that splits data based on certain conditions to predict outcomes. It consists of:

  • Root Node – The starting point of decision-making.
  • Internal Nodes – Decision-making points.
  • Leaf Nodes – Final predicted outcomes.

Example: Predicting whether a student will pass an exam based on study hours and attendance.
